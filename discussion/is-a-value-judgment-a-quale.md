# Is a value judgment — "this has social value" — a quale?

## The Question

If I think "this thing, activity, or behavior has value for society," does that thought constitute qualia?

## Short Answer

**The thought itself is not straightforwardly qualia, but the felt qualities accompanying it likely are.** The key is distinguishing the *content* of a thought from the *experience* of having it.

---

## Two Layers of Any Thought

| Layer | Description | Qualia? |
|---|---|---|
| **Propositional content** | The meaning: "this has social value" | ❌ Not in itself |
| **Phenomenal character** | What it *feels like* to think it — the felt sense of conviction, weight, or clarity | ✅ Yes |

The propositional content could in principle run "in the dark" — processed without any felt quality, like a logical operation in a computer. But the *feeling* of the thought — the sense that something matters, the weight of a moral judgment — has a subjective quality.

---

## What Chalmers' List Suggests

Chalmers explicitly includes thought-like experiences in his list of qualia:

> "from hard-edged pains to the elusive experience of thoughts on the tip of one's tongue"

A value judgment shares this character: the felt sense of recognizing importance, or of something "clicking" as meaningful, is phenomenal — there is something it is like to have it.

---

## The Contested Question

Whether *purely cognitive* states — thoughts stripped of any emotional or felt dimension — have phenomenal character at all is actively debated:

- **Cognitive phenomenology** (Strawson, Siewert): yes, all conscious thoughts have some phenomenal quality, even purely intellectual ones
- **Skeptics**: phenomenal character belongs only to sensory/emotional states; cognition itself is "dark"

Chalmers' own view leans toward there being *some* phenomenal character even in thought, but the debate is unresolved.

---

## Conclusion

A value judgment like "this has social value" likely involves qualia in the form of:
- The **felt sense of conviction** or certainty
- The **emotional weight** of caring about something
- The **phenomenal quality of understanding** — the "aha" of a thought coming together

Whether the abstract cognitive content itself (independent of any feeling) is phenomenal remains an open question in philosophy of mind.
