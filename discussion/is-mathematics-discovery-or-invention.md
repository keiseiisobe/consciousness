# Is mathematics discovery or invention?

## The Question

When mathematicians arrive at their results, are they:

- **(a) Discovering** truths that already exist independently of any mind — eternal structures that were always there, waiting to be found?
- **(b) Inventing** elaborate mental constructions — powerful and elegant, but with no existence outside of human thought?

---

## Penrose's Position: It Depends on What Comes Out

Penrose (*The Emperor's New Mind*) argues that the right answer varies case by case, and the test is:

> Does more come out of the structure than was put into it in the first place?

- **If yes → discovery.** When Cardano introduced complex numbers (~1539), he had no inkling of the Cauchy integral formula, the Riemann mapping theorem, or the Lewy extension property. These were not built in — they emerged from the structure itself, with no additional modifications. Such cases feel like "stumbling upon works of God."

- **If no → invention.** When a mathematician introduces a contrived construction mid-proof to achieve a narrow goal — and nothing more ever comes of it — the word *invention* is more apt. These are "works of man."

On this view, *discovery* is the greater achievement. Great mathematical discoveries have the same character as great art: a feeling, not uncommon among artists, that in their greatest works they are revealing eternal truths with a prior, ethereal existence.

---

## Einstein's Position: Certainty Requires Disconnection from Reality

Einstein (*Geometry and Experience*, 1921) turns the question around. He is not asking whether math feels like discovery — he is asking why mathematics is *certain* at all.

His answer: mathematical axioms, properly understood (following modern axiomatics), are **free creations of the human mind**. They carry no intuitive or empirical content. Schlick called them "implicit definitions" — they define the very objects they describe, rather than describing independently existing things.

> "As far as the laws of mathematics refer to reality, they are not certain; and as far as they are certain, they do not refer to reality."

The mystery of mathematics' effectiveness at describing the world is therefore not a sign of Platonic reality — it is a sign that we *select and adapt* the mathematical structures that fit, discarding those that don't.

---

## The Arguments in Conflict

| | Penrose | Einstein |
|---|---|---|
| **What is an axiom?** | A reflection of eternal mathematical truth | A free human creation, void of content until applied |
| **Why does math work?** | Mathematical structures are real — reality participates in them | We choose the math that works and revise when it fails |
| **Certainty** | Comes from the structures being real | Comes from disconnection from reality |
| **The mystery** | Deepened — why does eternal math describe the world? | Dissolved — there is no pre-established harmony |

---

## Why This Matters for Consciousness

Penrose extends his Platonist argument directly to the question of whether minds are computational:

1. Gödel's incompleteness theorems show that no algorithm can prove all true mathematical statements within a sufficiently rich formal system.
2. Human mathematicians can *see* that certain statements are true even when they cannot be proved algorithmically.
3. This seeing requires access to Platonic mathematical truth — something no algorithm can provide.
4. Therefore, consciousness cannot be reduced to computation.

Einstein's challenge cuts in a different direction: any mathematical theory of consciousness (IIT, Orch-OR) that achieves formal precision does so only by abstracting away from the actual experience — the very thing it is trying to explain.

---

## Open Questions

- If mathematical structures are discovered, is consciousness itself a mathematical structure — something that physical systems *participate in* rather than produce?
- If axioms are free human inventions, does IIT's Φ describe something real, or is it a useful fiction?
- Can Penrose's Gödel argument actually establish that human cognition is non-algorithmic, or does it only show that mathematicians *believe* they see beyond algorithms?
