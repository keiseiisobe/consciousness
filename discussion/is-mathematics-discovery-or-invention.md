# Is mathematics discovery or invention?

## The Question

When mathematicians arrive at their results, are they:

- **(a) Discovering** truths that already exist independently of any mind — eternal structures that were always there, waiting to be found?
- **(b) Inventing** elaborate mental constructions — powerful and elegant, but with no existence outside of human thought?

---

## Penrose's Position: It Depends on What Comes Out

Penrose (*The Emperor's New Mind*) argues that the right answer varies case by case, and the test is:

> Does more come out of the structure than was put into it in the first place?

- **If yes → discovery.** When Cardano introduced complex numbers (~1539), he had no inkling of the Cauchy integral formula, the Riemann mapping theorem, or the Lewy extension property. These were not built in — they emerged from the structure itself, with no additional modifications. Such cases feel like "stumbling upon works of God."

- **If no → invention.** When a mathematician introduces a contrived construction mid-proof to achieve a narrow goal — and nothing more ever comes of it — the word *invention* is more apt. These are "works of man."

On this view, *discovery* is the greater achievement. Great mathematical discoveries have the same character as great art: a feeling, not uncommon among artists, that in their greatest works they are revealing eternal truths with a prior, ethereal existence.

---

## Einstein's Position: Separate the Formal System from Its Interpretation

Einstein (*Geometry and Experience*, 1921) turns the question around. He is not asking whether math feels like discovery — he is asking why mathematics is *certain* at all.

His answer begins by separating **pure axiomatic geometry** from **practical geometry**. In the former, mathematical axioms are **free creations of the human mind**. They carry no intuitive or empirical content. Schlick called them “implicit definitions” — they define the very objects they describe, rather than describing independently existing things.

> "As far as the laws of mathematics refer to reality, they are not certain; and as far as they are certain, they do not refer to reality."

To say something about the world, however, we add coordinating assumptions connecting formal “points” and “lines” to rods, clocks, light rays, and physical laws. Einstein calls attention to the combined package:

> **G + P** = geometry plus the relevant physical laws and coordinating assumptions.

It is this package, not geometry alone, that experience can test. Einstein therefore does not merely say that we invent mathematics and discard whatever does not fit. He shows that evidence can underdetermine which part of an interconnected formal-and-physical system should be revised.

---

## The Arguments in Conflict

| | Penrose | Einstein |
|---|---|---|
| **What is an axiom?** | A reflection of eternal mathematical truth | A free human creation, void of content until applied |
| **Why does math work?** | Mathematical structures are real — reality participates in them | Formal structures gain physical content through coordinating assumptions |
| **Certainty** | Comes from the structures being real | Comes from disconnection from reality |
| **What experience tests** | Whether reality instantiates the discovered structure | The combined system G + P, not the formalism in isolation |

---

## Why This Matters for Consciousness

Penrose extends his Platonist argument directly to the question of whether minds are computational:

1. Gödel's incompleteness theorems show that no algorithm can prove all true mathematical statements within a sufficiently rich formal system.
2. Human mathematicians can *see* that certain statements are true even when they cannot be proved algorithmically.
3. This seeing requires access to Platonic mathematical truth — something no algorithm can provide.
4. Therefore, consciousness cannot be reduced to computation.

Einstein's challenge cuts in a different direction: formal precision alone cannot show that a variable refers to experience. IIT, Orch-OR, or any other mathematical theory must state the bridge principles that connect formal objects to reports, behavior, neural interventions, and first-person experience. Those principles are empirical commitments, not theorems of the formalism.

Hammack's proof framework helps keep several claims apart:

- A proof of **if P, then Q** establishes Q only under P; it does not establish that P describes the brain or the world.
- A definition can make a concept exact without showing that it corresponds to consciousness.
- Showing that two descriptions are equivalent requires both directions, not only that one implies the other.
- A counterexample can refute a universal claim, while many confirming cases do not deductively prove one.
- A non-constructive existence proof need not provide a procedure for finding or detecting the thing shown to exist.

Applied to IIT, deriving Φ from a system description may be mathematically valid while the assertion “Φ is consciousness” remains a bridge principle requiring independent defense. Applied to Penrose, Gödel's theorem is a result about formal systems; moving from it to a conclusion about what human minds can do requires further premises about soundness, consistency, and the mind's relation to any particular formal system.

## Open Questions

- If mathematical structures are discovered, is consciousness itself a mathematical structure — something that physical systems *participate in* rather than produce?
- If axioms are free human inventions, does IIT's Φ describe something real, or is it a useful fiction?
- Can Penrose's Gödel argument actually establish that human cognition is non-algorithmic, or does it only show that mathematicians *believe* they see beyond algorithms?
- What are the explicit G-like formal components and P-like bridge principles in each mathematical theory of consciousness?
- What observation would count as a counterexample to the bridge principle “Φ corresponds to consciousness”?
