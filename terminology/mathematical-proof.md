# Mathematical Proof

## Definition

A **mathematical proof** is a finite argument that derives a statement from stated assumptions, definitions, axioms, and previously established results by accepted rules of inference. Proof supplies deductive warrant: if the premises are granted and the reasoning is valid, the conclusion must follow.

This strength also marks its boundary. A proof can establish what follows **inside a formal or mathematical framework**; by itself it cannot establish that the framework's primitives correspond to consciousness, brains, or the physical world.

## Core Distinctions

Richard Hammack's *Book of Proof* provides a useful vocabulary for evaluating arguments in consciousness studies:

| Form | What it establishes | Common misuse in consciousness debates |
|---|---|---|
| **Definition** | Fixes how a term will be used | Treating a stipulated definition of consciousness as an empirical discovery |
| **Conditional proof** | If P, then Q | Concluding Q without establishing P in real systems |
| **Biconditional proof** | P if and only if Q; both directions | Showing a neural property is sufficient and silently treating it as necessary too |
| **Existence proof** | At least one object satisfies a condition | Assuming the proof supplies a way to construct or detect it |
| **Constructive proof** | Establishes existence by exhibiting or generating an example | Confusing a model instance with an observed biological instance |
| **Counterexample** | Refutes a universal claim with one case | Ignoring edge cases because the theory fits many typical cases |
| **Induction** | Establishes an indexed infinity of mathematical statements | Confusing mathematical induction with empirical generalization from samples |

## Formal Validity vs. Empirical Truth

Einstein's distinction in *Geometry and Experience* gives a model for applying mathematics to consciousness:

```text
F = formal system: definitions, axioms, equations, derivations
B = bridge principles: how formal terms connect to experience and measurement
E = empirical procedures and observations

F alone can be proved internally.
F + B is confronted with E.
```

For a geometrical theory, bridge principles connect formal lines and intervals to rods, clocks, and light. For a theory of consciousness, they might connect a mathematical quantity to verbal report, perceptual discrimination, neural activity, anesthesia, or first-person structure.

The bridge principles are indispensable. Without them, a mathematically impeccable theory may have no determined empirical meaning. With them, the theory becomes testable—but the certainty of pure proof no longer transfers automatically to the claim about reality.

## Applications

### Integrated Information Theory

A derivation of Φ from a formally described system can prove a fact about that model. The further assertion that Φ measures, constitutes, or is identical with consciousness is not obtained from the derivation alone. It depends on definitions and bridge principles that must be defended conceptually and empirically.

### Penrose and Gödel

Gödel's incompleteness theorems prove limits for suitable formal systems. An argument from those theorems to “human mathematical understanding is non-computational” needs additional premises: which formal system models a mind, whether the thinker can justifiably know that system to be sound or consistent, and whether human mathematical judgment is itself reliable in the required way. Separating theorem from bridge premises makes the philosophical dispute visible.

### Neural Correlates

Evidence that a neural state N reliably accompanies reported experience C supports a correlation. It does not by itself prove either direction of the biconditional `N ↔ C`, much less the identity `N = C`. Necessity, sufficiency, identity, constitution, and causal production are different claims and require different arguments.

## A Checklist for Claims

When a consciousness argument uses mathematics, ask:

1. What exactly is defined, assumed, and proved?
2. Is the conclusion conditional, biconditional, existential, or universal?
3. Which premises are mathematical, and which are empirical or philosophical?
4. What bridge principles connect the symbols to experience or measurement?
5. Would one counterexample refute the claim?
6. Does the evidence show necessity, sufficiency, both, or neither?
